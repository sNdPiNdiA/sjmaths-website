Chapter 7: Coordinate Geometry

Board Exam Focused Notes, Formulas, and PYQs

Exam Weightage & Blueprint

Total: 6 Marks

This chapter is a standalone unit. It's high scoring because it is purely formula-based.

Question Type Marks Frequency Focus Topic
MCQ 1 High Distance from Origin, Midpoint
Short Answer 2 or 3 Medium Ratio Problems (Section Formula)
Case Study 4 Medium Real life scenarios (Distance/Section)

‚è∞ Last 24-Hour Checklist

  • Distance Formula: $\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}$.
  • Distance from Origin: $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$.
  • Section Formula: Internal Division.
  • Midpoint Formula: $(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2})$.
  • Centroid Formula: $(\frac{x_1+x_2+x_3}{3}, \frac{y_1+y_2+y_3}{3})$.
  • Collinear Condition: Area of Triangle = 0.

Important Formulas üî•üî•üî•

1. Distance Formula

The distance between two points $P(x_1, y_1)$ and $Q(x_2, y_2)$ is:

$$ d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} $$
Special Case: Distance of $P(x,y)$ from Origin $(0,0)$ is $\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$.

2. Section Formula

The coordinates of point $P(x, y)$ dividing the line segment joining $A(x_1, y_1)$ and $B(x_2, y_2)$ internally in ratio $m_1:m_2$ are:

$$ x = \frac{m_1x_2 + m_2x_1}{m_1 + m_2}, \quad y = \frac{m_1y_2 + m_2y_1}{m_1 + m_2} $$

3. Mid-Point Formula

If $P$ is the mid-point (ratio 1:1), then:

$$ x = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \quad y = \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} $$

Identifying Quadrilaterals ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ

To identify the type of quadrilateral formed by 4 points, calculating distances (sides and diagonals) is key.

Properties Found Quadrilateral Type
All sides equal + Diagonals equal Square
All sides equal + Diagonals unequal Rhombus
Opposite sides equal + Diagonals equal Rectangle
Opposite sides equal + Diagonals unequal Parallelogram
‚ö†Ô∏è Caution: Don't stop after checking sides! You MUST check diagonals to distinguish between Square/Rhombus and Rectangle/Parallelogram.

Solved Examples (Board Marking Scheme)

Q1. Find the ratio in which the y-axis divides the line segment joining $(5, -6)$ and $(-1, -4)$. Also find the point of intersection. (3 Marks)

Step 1: Assumption 0.5 Mark

Let ratio be $k:1$. Let point on y-axis be $P(0, y)$.

Step 2: Use Section Formula for x 1 Mark

$x = \frac{m_1x_2 + m_2x_1}{m_1 + m_2}$

$0 = \frac{k(-1) + 1(5)}{k + 1}$

$-k + 5 = 0 \Rightarrow k = 5$. Ratio is 5:1.

Step 3: Find y 1 Mark

$y = \frac{5(-4) + 1(-6)}{5 + 1} = \frac{-20 - 6}{6} = \frac{-26}{6} = \frac{-13}{3}$.

Step 4: Conclusion 0.5 Mark

Ratio is 5:1. Point is $(0, -13/3)$.

Q2. Find a point on the x-axis which is equidistant from $(2, -5)$ and $(-2, 9)$. (3 Marks)

Step 1: Setup 0.5 Mark

Let point on x-axis be $P(x, 0)$. Let $A(2, -5)$ and $B(-2, 9)$.

Given $PA = PB \Rightarrow PA^2 = PB^2$.

Step 2: Apply Distance Formula 1.5 Marks

$(x-2)^2 + (0 - (-5))^2 = (x - (-2))^2 + (0 - 9)^2$

$(x-2)^2 + 25 = (x+2)^2 + 81$

$x^2 - 4x + 4 + 25 = x^2 + 4x + 4 + 81$

Step 3: Solve 1 Mark

$-4x + 29 = 4x + 85$

$-8x = 56 \Rightarrow x = -7$.

Point is $(-7, 0)$.

Previous Year Questions (PYQs)

2023 (1 Mark): Distance of point $P(3, 4)$ from x-axis is?
Ans: 4 units. (Distance from x-axis = y-coordinate absolute value).
2020 (3 Marks): Find the ratio in which $P(4, m)$ divides the line segment joining $A(2, 3)$ and $B(6, -3)$. Hence find m.
Ans: Ratio $1:1$ (Midpoint). $m = 0$.
2019 (3 Marks): Find the coordinates of points of trisection of the line segment joining $(4, -1)$ and $(-2, -3)$.
Ans: Ratios are $1:2$ and $2:1$. Points: $(2, -5/3)$ and $(0, -7/3)$.

Exam Strategy & Mistake Bank

Mistake Bank üö®

Formula Sign: In section formula, students often write minus instead of plus in the denominator.
Square Root: $\sqrt{x^2+y^2} \neq x+y$. Do not cancel the root with squares inside a sum!
Equidistant: Equidistant does NOT mean Midpoint unless the points are collinear. Use Distance Formula, not Section Formula.

Scoring Tips üèÜ

Assume Ratio: Always assume ratio as $k:1$ instead of $m_1:m_2$. It reduces variables and makes calculation easier.
Draw Figure: A rough sketch helps visualize quadrants and relative positions.
Squaring: In distance problems ($PA=PB$), square both sides ($PA^2=PB^2$) to eliminate square roots early.
