Chapter 9: Circles

HOTS Level Worksheet

Back to Worksheets
  1. Question 1: Prove that the quadrilateral formed by the angle bisectors of a cyclic quadrilateral is also cyclic.
    Solution: Let the cyclic quadrilateral be ABCD. Let the angle bisectors form quadrilateral PQRS. We need to show PQRS is cyclic. This is a complex proof involving angle chasing. Let the bisectors of $\angle A$ and $\angle B$ meet at P, $\angle B$ and $\angle C$ at Q, etc. In $\triangle APB$, $\angle APB = 180 - (\frac{A}{2} + \frac{B}{2})$. Similarly, in $\triangle CRD$, $\angle CRD = 180 - (\frac{C}{2} + \frac{D}{2})$. The sum of opposite angles $\angle APB + \angle CRD = 360 - \frac{1}{2}(A+B+C+D) = 360 - \frac{1}{2}(360) = 180^\circ$. A quadrilateral is cyclic if the sum of opposite angles is $180^\circ$. So PQRS is cyclic.
  2. Question 2: Two circles intersect at two points B and C. Through B, two line segments ABD and PBQ are drawn to intersect the circles at A, D and P, Q respectively. Prove that $\angle ACP = \angle QCD$.
    Solution: For the first circle, $\angle ACP$ and $\angle ABP$ are angles in the same segment. So $\angle ACP = \angle ABP$. For the second circle, $\angle QCD$ and $\angle QBD$ are angles in the same segment, so $\angle QCD = \angle QBD$. Since ABD and PBQ are straight lines, $\angle ABP$ and $\angle QBD$ are vertically opposite angles, so they are equal. Therefore, $\angle ACP = \angle QCD$.
  3. Question 3: If the non-parallel sides of a trapezium are equal, prove that it is cyclic.
    Solution: Let ABCD be a trapezium with $AB || DC$ and $AD=BC$. Draw perpendiculars from D and C to AB, let them be DM and CN. In $\triangle DMA$ and $\triangle CNB$, $AD=BC$ (given), $DM=CN$ (distance between parallels). By RHS, $\triangle DMA \cong \triangle CNB$. So $\angle A = \angle B$. Also, $\angle A + \angle D = 180^\circ$ and $\angle B + \angle C = 180^\circ$ (consecutive interior angles). Since $\angle A = \angle B$, this implies $\angle C = \angle D$. Now $\angle A + \angle C = \angle B + \angle C = 180^\circ$. Since a pair of opposite angles is supplementary, the trapezium is cyclic.
  4. Question 4: Prove that the circle drawn with any side of a rhombus as diameter, passes through the point of intersection of its diagonals.
    Solution: Let the rhombus be ABCD and diagonals intersect at O. Diagonals of a rhombus bisect each other at right angles. So $\angle AOB = 90^\circ$. A circle drawn with side AB as diameter will have the angle in the semicircle as $90^\circ$. Since $\angle AOB=90^\circ$, the point O must lie on this circle.
  5. Question 5: Prove that the line of centres of two intersecting circles subtends equal angles at the two points of intersection.
    Solution: Let the centers be O and O' and intersecting points be A and B. We need to prove $\angle OAO' = \angle OBO'$. In $\triangle OAO'$ and $\triangle OBO'$, $OA=OB$ (radii of first circle), $O'A=O'B$ (radii of second circle), and OO' is common. By SSS congruence, $\triangle OAO' \cong \triangle OBO'$. Therefore, $\angle OAO' = \angle OBO'$ (CPCTC).
  6. Question 6: If circles are drawn taking two sides of a triangle as diameters, prove that the point of intersection of these circles lie on the third side.
    Solution: Let the triangle be ABC. Circles are drawn with AB and AC as diameters. Let them intersect at D. The angle in a semicircle is $90^\circ$. So, $\angle ADB = 90^\circ$ and $\angle ADC = 90^\circ$. Adding these, $\angle ADB + \angle ADC = 180^\circ$. This means BDC is a straight line. So, the point of intersection D lies on the third side BC.
  7. Question 7: ABC is a triangle and P is a point on the side BC. A circle is drawn through B, P and a point Q on AB and another circle is drawn through C, P, R where R is a point on AC. If the circles intersect at S, prove that S lies on the circumcircle of triangle AQR.
    Solution: This is a more advanced theorem, often related to Miquel's point. Considering the cyclic quadrilateral BQSP, we have $\angle BSQ = 180 - \angle B$. Also in cyclic quad PRSC, $\angle PSR = 180 - \angle C$. Then $\angle QSR = 360 - (180-\angle B) - (180-\angle C) = \angle B + \angle C$. In $\triangle ABC$, $\angle A = 180 - (\angle B + \angle C)$. Thus $\angle QSR = 180 - \angle A$. This means the quadrilateral AQSR has opposite angles supplementary, so it is cyclic. Hence S lies on the circumcircle of $\triangle AQR$.
  8. Question 8: Prove that the angle bisector of any angle of a triangle and the perpendicular bisector of the opposite side intersect on the circumcircle of the triangle.
    Solution: Let the angle bisector of $\angle A$ of $\triangle ABC$ intersect the circumcircle at D. Join DB and DC. Since angles subtended by the same arc are equal, $\angle DBC = \angle DAC = A/2$. Also $\angle DCB = \angle DAB = A/2$. So $\triangle DBC$ is isosceles with $DB=DC$. The perpendicular bisector of BC must pass through D, which is the vertex of the isosceles triangle. Hence the intersection point lies on the circumcircle.
